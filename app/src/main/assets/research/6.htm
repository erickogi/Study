Population and Sampling

In addition to refining concepts and measurements, you must decide who and what to study.

The population for a study is that group (usually of people)about whom we want to draw conclusions. We are never able to study all the members of a population…reasons?

We select a sample from the population. Note that decisions about population and sampling are related to the research method to be used..meaning?

Whereas probability sampling techniques would be relevant to a large scale survey or content analysis, a field researcher might need to select only those informants who would yield a balanced of the situation under study and an experimenter might assign subjects to experimental and control groups in a manner that ensures comparability.

Observations

Having decided what to study among whom by what method, you’re now ready to make observations--to collect empirical data. Will you use questionnaires or interviews? The advantages and disadvantages of these will be discussed in later lessons.

Data Processing

The data collected will now need to be processed.

For example, the data processing phase for a survey typically involves the classification (coding) of written in answers and the transfer of all information to a computer.

Analysis

Finally, the collected data has to be interpreted in order to draw conclusions that reflect the interests, ideas and theories that initiated the inquiry.

Note that the results of the analysis will feed back into the initial interests, ideas and theories. In practice, this feedback may initiate another kind of enquiry (areas forfurther research).

Application

This is the final stage of the research process It involves the uses made of the research and the

conclusions arrived at.

Start by communicating the findings through a report or some other means, oral presentation-eg in a conference

One can go further to not only report but also to discuss the implications of the findings-especially for decision making.
