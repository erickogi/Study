INTRODUCTION TO RADIO AND TELEVISION COMMUNICATION

Definition of Terms

Radio: An electronic device designed to receive, demodulate, and amplify radio signals from sound broadcasting stations, etc. or an electronic device permitting both transmission and reception of radio signals for two-way communications.

Television (TV): Broadcasting visual images of stationary or moving objects or an electronic device that receives television signals and displays them on a screen.

Communication: Communication is giving, receiving or exchanging ideas, information, signals or messages through appropriate media, enabling individuals or groups to persuade, to seek information, to give information or to express emotions.

Broadcasting: Wikipedia (2010) defines broadcasting as the distribution of audio and video content to a dispersed audience via radio, television, or other, often digital transmission media.
According to Nwadigwe (2006) broadcasting is the sharing of information through electronic devices. Broadcast by television medium is known as telecast.

Characteristics of Radio

Radio has speed. It is the fastest in terms of giving the breaking news. It can easily provide something new and surprise the listener.
When people tune to a radio they know what to expect but creative and stimulating producers can give something new.
Radio can move with people’s feelings or moods i.e. where a caller makes a request following their suffering.
The fact that the presenter cannot be seen allows them the freedom to surprise listeners with the kind of things said.

Radio can suffer from interference. Radio can easily suffer fading and co-channel may vary the AM at night.
Quality of sound received may vary from that carefully produced and balanced in the studio.
Reception in a moving vehicle is difficult due to varying signal strength.

Radio has no boundaries. Radio does not respect territorial limits. Its signals can go over mountains and across oceans.
It can transcend the territorial boundaries and link up different communities or nationalities. Print media can be stopped at national frontiers.

Radio is local and portable. Can speak all languages. It can operate within a small locality and address the needs of a community and speak their language. It is small in size and one can move around with it.

Radio is a secondary medium. Radio can be used as background while the listener is busy engaging in other activities.
It is less demanding as it permits one to undertake other things at the same time. This generosity makes it interruptible. This leads to low level of commitment on the part of the listeners.

Radio is a simple technology. It requires very simple technology including the mixer, microphone, transmission equipment and computer system.
This technology is simple to operate and even non-professionals can be involved.
These simplicity allows for flexibility in programming since changes on programs can be done on short notice.

Radio is cheap. The capital and running cost for radio and radio stations is relatively cheap. In many countries getting a transmission frequency may be more difficult than having the finance.
Frequencies are not easily assigned.
The cost of buying a radio and operating it as an audience is low thus attracting a substantial audience. The large number of audience makes the cost per listener for advertisers and sponsors low.
Radio is cheaper than books, newspapers, TV, Internet and mobile phone.

Radio is highly segmented. The low cost of putting the radio station has encouraged many investors.
To cut out a market for your communication product requires segmentation of the mass. This tough competition for audience has led to a highly segmented audience that may imply small audience size for each radio station.

Radio is transient in nature. The medium demands you must be there to get the content. If you miss details and do not understand everything it is gone and unless you wait for it to be repeated latter.
This encourages misinterpretation and taking of content out of context.
The broadcaster has to keep a record of all content broadcasted in order to safeguard against allegations of malpractice. The transient nature requires that producers and presenters have clear ideas and use simple language.

Radio lacks space. If a newspaper has a lot of material it can increase its pagination to create space. This will encourage detailed coverage. However, with radio you cannot increase time to create space for more material.
An increase in one material will mean removing some programs as time is a constant (24 hours)

Radio makes pictures.
 Radio stimulates imagination. Listeners use the voices to visualize what they hear and the bearer of the voice. The pictures are limited by the use of the size of one’s imagination. However, the visions are liable to distortions due to selectivity.
The radio writer must choose words that will create the right picture in the listener’s mind.

Radio speaks to individuals. It is a narrow casting medium.
Radio is personal and can speak directly to the listeners.
One can listen to the radio as an individual and be linked to other people who are listening especially if it is a live transmission. For example, greetings.

Radio speaks to many. It is a mass medium. Radio has a large reach as many people own the radio since it is cheap.

Functions of Radio/TV

1. Agenda setting.
The media guides people to identify important issues that need their decision making through highlighting them.
They raise personal, economic, political, military, ideological, diplomatic, cultural and social issues to national levels.

2. Commercial.
Advertisers pay to access the audience thus providing major revenue for the media and the government through taxes.

3. Correlation.
The media correlates various parts of society so as to respond to the environment.
Correlation involves: Explain, interpret, and comment on the meaning and consequence of events and information; Consensus building; Provide support for established authority and norm; Setting orders of priority and signaling relative status

4. Education.
On the policies of governance, and on the rights and responsibilities of citizenry could be carried through the media.
Education involves impartation of knowledge, skills and attitude to the audience e.g. cooking programmes.

5. Entertainment.
Media entertains the public by providing content that can amuse, show emotional release, relaxation, killing time or boredom, sexual release and momentary escape from problems.

6. Information providing.
The media through its surveillance ability is able to collect and distribute information within and outside a particular environment.
The information flow is necessary for unity in society and in solving people’s problems and making decision.

7. Persuasion.
Used to influence change by advertisers and informational campaigners.

8. Promote development. Is the ability to improve the quality of life by:Enhancing people’s level of participation in development; Increasing social awareness; Mobilizing people for development

9. Social Responsibility Media protects society from harm by exposing potential threats to social order.
It promotes national cohesion in society. Media preserves society’s cultural value from external influence.

10. Transmission of social or cultural heritage. This involves transmission of cultural heritage from one generation to another. It entails expressing the dominant culture and recognizing subcultures and new cultural development.
