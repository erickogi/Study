EVALUATION OF EFFICENCY AND EFFECTIVENESS OF MIS

Efficiency and Effectiveness of Management Information Systems

--Efficiency is the relationship between the level of performance of a system or product and the amount of resources used, under stated conditions.

--Increasing efficiency is a matter of achieving the same outputs with fewer resources or more outputs for the same amount of resources.

--Effectiveness, on the other hand, is ability of information system to achieve stated goals or objectives, judged in terms of both output and impact. It is a measure of how well an information system works, and the extent to which it achieves its intended results

Measures of Effective and Efficient Information Systems

Evaluation of a MIS is done to determine:

--Technical feasibility – availability of needed technology

--Operational feasibility – fitting proposed solutions with existing operations

--Economic feasibility – availability of funds to implement proposed solutions

Criteria for Evaluating MIS. Includes:

1.Maintainability

2.Reliability

3.Robustness

4.Availability

5.Acceptability

6.Documentation

7.Cohesiveness

8.Functionality

9.Efficiency

10.User interface

INFORMATION SYSTEM SECURITY

--As organizations become increasingly dependent on information systems for strategic advantage and operations, information systems security also becomes increasingly important.

--Through communications networks, the potential for unauthorized access, abuse, or fraud can occur at any access point in the network.

--Information systems threats can stem from technical, organizational, and environmental factors, compounded by poor management decisions

Classification of Security Attacks

i. Passive attacks:

--Eavesdropping or monitoring of transmissions

--Release of message contents

--Traffic analysis

--Phishing attacks.

--Social engineering

ii. Active Attacks

--These involve falsification of data and transactions. Includes:

--Masquerade

--Replay

--Modification of messages

--Hacking

--Cybervandalism

--Malicious software programs

--Computer abuse

--Denial of service

How to Deal With Systems Security Problems

a. Procedural

--Risk assessment. This determines the level of risk to the organization

--Develop and issue an information system security policy

--Raise awareness among information system actors

--Taking responsibility and being accountable for the security of participants’ own information system

--Ensuring observance of computing ethics at the workplace.

--Invest in information system security to prevent abuses

--Other commonsense measures e.g. backing up data

b. Physical

--Emergency power source (UPS)/Power surge protectors

--Restricted access to data/computer room

c. Logical

--Security design and implementation should be incorporated as a basic element of any system.

--Fault-tolerant computer systems.

--Access control e.g. use of passwords.

--Biometric authentication.

--Digital signature

--Firewalls, intrusion detection systems, and anti-virus software.

--Encryption

Organizational constraints in dealing with security issues

--Lack of staff with specific security expertise

--Inadequate financial resources to hire consultants or provide staff training

--Lack of understanding or being dismissive of the risks, and

--Inability to focus upon security due to other business priorities.

MANAGING KNOWLEDGE

 --Knowledge is most useful and actionable when it is shared throughout the firm. Knowledge has several dimensions that must be understood.

--It is an intangible asset and is not subject to the law of diminishing returns as are physical assets, but it experiences network effects as its value increases as more people share it.

--The transformation of data into useful information and knowledge requires organizational resources.

Knowledge has different forms.

--It can be either tacit or explicit and it involves know-how, craft, and skill.

--It involves knowing how to follow procedures and knowing why, not simply when, things happen.

--Knowledge has location. It is a cognitive event involving mental models and maps of individuals, having both a social and an individual basis of it. It is “sticky” (hard to move), situated, and contextual.

--knowledge is situational. It is conditional, knowing when to apply a procedure is just as important as knowing the procedure. It is related to context, meaning it must be known how to use a certain tool and under what circumstances.

--Organizations create and gather knowledge using a variety of organizational learning mechanisms.

--Organizational learning is the process of learning to adjust the organization’s behavior to reflect learning by creating new business processes and by changing patterns of management decision making.

--Organizations that can sense and respond to their environments rapidly will survive longer than organizations that have poor learning mechanisms.

--Knowledge management is the set of processes developed in an organization to create, gather, store, maintain, and disseminate the firm’s knowledge. It increases the ability of the organization to learn from its environment and to incorporate knowledge into its business processes.

--To maximize the return on investment in knowledge management projects, supportive values, structures, and behavior patterns must be built. Knowledge management involves both information systems activities and a host of enabling management and organizational activities

Information system activities include:-

--knowledge acquisition (knowledge discovery, data mining, neural networks, genetic algorithms, knowledge workstations, expert knowledge networks),

--knowledge storage (document management systems, knowledge databases, expert systems), knowledge dissemination (Intranet portals, push email reports, search engines, collaboration), and knowledge application (decision support systems, enterprise applications
