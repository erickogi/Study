COMMUNICATION AND DECSION-MAKING

Decision Making Process

--Decision-making is the process of making an informed choice, or selecting a course of action, from among the alternative actions that are possible.

--It is an integral part of management and occurs in every function and at all levels.

--It is no longer confined to middle and top-level management as was the case in previous years; today operational employees are also responsible for some decision-making because information systems make information available to all levels.

--Types of decisions taken vary by management level, but they all go through a similar, iterative process as explained below.

Four Steps in Decision-Making

--Intelligence – a manager tries to discover, identify, and understand the problems occurring in the organization – why is there a problem and what effects is it having on the firm? One recognizes that a decision is necessary; that it must be made. Your awareness may be triggered by a variety of things: the need to change business focus, pressure from employees or customers, etc. You then try to define clearly the nature of the decision you must make. You must define and clarify the issue: does it warrant action? If so, now or later? Etc.

--Design. Involves identifying and exploring various solutions to problems by:

Gathering relevant information - know what information is needed information, and how to get it.

-- Identify alternatives - through the information collected, or by using your imagination and information collected, or you may brainstorm within the organization.

– Weigh evidence - evaluate whether the problem identified in Step 1 will be solved through the use of each alternative. i.e., consider and compare the pros and cons of each option. Then place the available alternatives in priority order, based upon some value system.

Choice - select a course of action from those available, which seems to be best suited to solve the problem. You may even choose a combination of alternatives. Avoid vagueness.

Implementation - implement the alternative you chose. Inform all those who may be involved and affected, and follow up to ensure proper and effective implementation. Also evaluate whether or not the decision chosen has solved the problem.

Types of Decisions

--Decisions are categorized as structured (or programmed), semi-structured (or semi-programmed) and unstructured (or non-programmed).

--Unstructured decisions are those in which the decision-maker must use their own best judgment, evaluation, and insight to solve the problem. Such decisions are non-routine. They are characterized by a high degree of uncertainty and cannot be delegated to lower level management, e.g. approving capital budget; launching new products, etc. Answering these questions require access to internal and external sources of information

--Structured decisions are repetitive and routine, and involve a definite procedure for handling them so that they don’t have to be treated each time as if they were new. They are also often automated, e.g. and include determining special offers to customers, etc.

--Semi-structured decisions. These are decisions that have elements of both structured and unstructured decisions. Only part of the problem has clear-cut answer provided by an accepted procedure. E.g. preparing a departmental budget, or a corporate website, tactical managers will need to get reports and information from the firm’s information system (structured) as well as interview staff and customers (unstructured).

--The different management levels in an organization have different information requirements for decision support and responsibility for different types of decisions.

– At strategic level, information is largely external-oriented but may also be internal-oriented, depending on the decision to be made

– At middle level, information is both internal and external-oriented

– At operational level, information is largely internal-oriented

MIS and Decision Making

--One of the main contributions of MIS has been to improve decision-making.

--Decision-making is based on information. Information is the trigger to knowing there is a problem. Information is needed to define and structure the problem, to explore and choose between alternative solutions, and to review the effects of the implemented choice

 --Examples of how various MISs support the different levels of management to make different types of decisions:

--MIS provide routine reports and summaries of transaction-level data to middle level managers to provide answers to structured and semi-structured decision-problems.

--DSS provide analytical models or tools for analyzing large quantities of data and supportive interactive queries for middle managers who face semi-structured decision situations and senior managers who make unstructured decisions.

--ESS provide senior management with external information and high-level summaries of the firm’s performance, to make primarily unstructured decisions

--KWS provide tools for knowledge discovery, communication, and collaboration that make knowledge more easily available to decision makers and integrate it into the business processes of the firm.

 

Organizational Models of Decision

Making

--Models exist to help employees at all levels of management to make decisions. Some of the models are at the individual level of analysis and some are at the organizational level. Exploring both levels of analysis can be useful in building effective computerized MIS.

Common categories of decision making models are explained below.

 (i) Prescriptive (or normative or rational model)

--is one which automatically selects the best option.

--Usually developed by operational researchers, economists and management scientists.--It is well-defined and structured and treats decision making as an entirely rational process

--It is grounded on goals/objectives, alternatives, consequences and optimality

--It assumes that complete information regarding the decision to be made is available and one correct conception of a problem or decision to be made can be determined

--It assumes also that the decision-makers consistently assess the advantages and disadvantages of any alternatives with goals and objectives in mind and then evaluate the consequences of selecting or not selecting each alternative. The best alternative is then selected.

 

--It more suited to operational and tactical levels where there is less uncertainty

Major advantage. The model utilizes a logical, sequential approach. Decisions are made by determining the objectives to be obtained, evaluating the potential alternatives and choosing the “best” alternative.

Major disadvantage. It assumes (quite wrongly!) that there are no biases to the decision-making process

(ii)Descriptive decision model

--have been developed by behavioral scientists and seek to explain actual behaviour in decision-making.

--Actual decision-making is less structured and is not completely rational

--Decision makers take account of imperfections of knowledge and behaviour and, because of practical difficulties, are prepared to accept a solution as ‘satisfactory’

--Decision-makers are not fully aware of all the alternatives available nor is there a single, clear-cut objective. Decision-making is rational but within boundaries of imperfections of information (i.e., bounded rationality)

--This means that formal MIS should provide decision-makers with background information and ways of exploring alternatives rather than mechanical decision rules and procedures.

--It is very appropriate at top-level management

(iii)The Process model

--It is fairly structured. Decisions are made based upon standard operating procedures within the organization. Actions and behaviors occur in accordance with these procedures or guidelines

--Conformity is an integral part of the process model since it is the means by which doubt is dealt with during the decision task. If decision makers are uncertain as to the potential effectiveness or the results of a decision, they conform to the pre-established standard

 --Conformity is both an advantage and a disadvantage, e.g. if uncertain about what to do, follow established procedure. On the other hand, it is bureaucratic – following standard operating procedures can be very lengthy and so delays decision-making.

(iv)The Garbage Can Model

--This model is useful where the involvement of participants fluctuates in the amount of time and effort given, and choices are inconsistent and not well defined

--Here, an opportunity to make a decision is described as a garbage can into which many types of problems and solutions are dropped independently of each other by decision-makers as these problems and solutions are generated

--The problems, solutions and decision makers are not necessarily related to each other. They move from one decision opportunity to another in such a manner that the solutions, the time needed and the problems seem to rely on a chance alignment of components to complete the decision.

Advantage. The model provides a real-world representation of the non-rational manner in which decisions are often made within an organization. Not all decisions are made in a logical or standard fashion. Occasionally, decisions are made on an ad hoc basis when the solutions, problems and individuals involved in the task happen to align.

Disadvantage. It is not efficient means of making a decision

– choices are made only when the combination of problems, solutions and individuals allow the decision to happen (i.e., are in alignment). Also, it is accidental decision making.
