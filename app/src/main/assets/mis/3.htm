SYSTEMS FROM FUNCTIONAL PERSPECTIVE

This topic discusses typical information systems that support operations and management of selected organizational functions and provide examples of functional applications for each organizational level

 Types of systems

1.Sales and marketing information systems

2.Manufacturing and production information systems

3.Finance and accounting information system

4.Human resource systems

Sales and Marketing Information Systems (SMIS)

The sales and marketing function is responsible for selling the organization’s products or services. Marketing is concerned with: identifying customers’ needs, planning and developing products and services, etc

SMIS support these activities by:

a)At the strategic level, SMIS are used to monitor trends affecting new products and sales opportunities, support planning for new product and services, etc. Example of SMIS is: Sales Trend Forecasting Information System

b)At the tactical management level, SMIS are used to support market research, advertising and promotional campaigns, etc. Example of SMIS here is: Pricing Analysis Information System.

c)At the operational level, SMIS used to assist in locating and contacting prospective customers, tracking sales, processing orders, etc. Example of SMIS here is: Order Processing Information System.

Manufacturing and Production  Information Systems (MPIS)

The manufacturing and production function is responsible for actually producing an organization’s goods and services. Major functions of a manufacturing company include:

--Personnel/HR - recruiting and training employees and maintaining employee records and work-force planning.

--Production - activities directly involving the manufacture of products.

--Marketing - consists of the sales and sales promotion activities, and product pricing.

--Engineering - design the products, develop the specifications that must be followed during manufacture, and design the production system.

 --Distribution - movement of the company’s products to its marketing outlets.

--Accounting - maintains the records of the financial transactions, assets and liabilities; produces the financial statements, and runs the financial control systems.

--Purchasing - acquisition of raw materials, components, and supplies.

--Inventory management - maintains physical control over supplies, materials, components, and finished products.

--Information management - developing, operating, and maintaining the computer information system that accomplishes the data processing.

--Treasury - handles the organization’s financing activities; is concerned with its capital structure, and usually manages its cash.

 

MPIS support these functions by:

a)At the strategic level, MPIS deal with the firm’s long-term manufacturing goals, e.g. where to locate new plants, and whether to invest in new manufacturing technology

b)At the tactical management level, MPIS are used to analyze and monitor manufacturing costs and resources, and analyze and monitor production costs and resources

c)At the operational level, MPIS deal with the status of production tasks.

 

Finance and Accounting Information  Systems (FAIS)

--The finance function is responsible for: managing the firm’s financial assets, and managing the capitalization of the firm (finding new financial assets in stock, bonds, etc.).

--The accounting function is chiefly responsible for: maintaining the firm’s financial records to account for the flow of funds in a firm.

--Finance and accounting help to keep track of a firm’s financial assets and fund flows. They provide answers to questions such as: what is the current inventory of financial assets? what records exist for disbursements, receipts, pay roll, and other fund flows?

FAIS support these activities by:

a)At the strategic level they help to establish long-term investment goals for the firm and provide long-range forecasts of the firm’s financial performance.

b)At the Tactical level, they help to oversee and control the firm’s financial resources

c)At the operational level, FAIS help to track the flow of funds in the firm through transaction such as paychecks, payments to vendors, securities reports, and receipts

Human Resource Systems (HRIS)

The human resource function is responsible for attracting, developing, and maintaining the firm’s workforce.

HRIS support these activities by:

a)At the strategic level, HRIS help to identify the manpower requirements for meeting the firm’s long-term business plans.

b)At the tactical level, HRIS help managers monitor and analyze the recruitment, allocation, and compensation of employees.

c)At operational level, HRIS help to track the recruitment and placement of the firm’s employees A specific example to illustrate the working of a HRIS at operational level:

--A typical HRIS at operational level (i.e. human resources TPS for employees record keeping) does the following:

--It maintains basic employee data, such as the employee’s name, age sex, marital status, address, educational background, salary, job title, date of hire, etc.

--Produces a variety of reports, such as lists of newly hired employees, employees who are terminated or on leaves of absence, employees classified by job type or educational level, or employee job performance evaluations.

HUMAN BEHAVIOUR IN ORGANIZATIONS

IT is having a major impact on various aspects of organizations and the people who work in them. There is need to understand this impact in order to know how to manage it

Technology and People

We are living in a virtual frontier where there are opportunities as well as challenges. For example, many services and capabilities are becoming mainstream applications, e.g.:

Many enabling technologies help us in various ways and places, e.g. email is facilitating communication; online shopping is having a major impact on consumer buying habits, etc.

There is also digital convergence, e.g. government agencies are consolidating their efforts through digital convergence to enable e-government (i.e. government’s use of IT to exchange information and services with citizens, businesses, and other arms of government.

--Each day, new applications are being envisioned and created.

--The cumulative effects of these changes have altered the basic constructs of society and the way we live and work

 Impacts of Technology on People

From an organizational perspective, technology impacts on people in a number of ways. The main areas of influence include: Organizational structure, Individuals and jobs, and Organizational risk.

For individuals and jobs

--Technology alters the relationships between individuals and departments within the organization, e.g. international project teams work together via ICTs to create and support products such as automobiles.

--Technology changes jobs and the way they are done, e.g., it simplifies and reduces tasks needing manual skills and strength, especially in factories and all forms of production, it can increase productivity e.g. use of reprogrammable robots for such tasks as welding and spraying; reduces costs and improve quality and consistency of finished products.

--Technology alters the skills and requirements for individuals, e.g., routine tasks requiring low level skills are disappearing fast, such as the disappearance of the copy typist due to availability of word-processors and printers. This means we must constantly update our skills in order to remain relevant.

--The Internet has enabled us to telecommute

 For organizational structure

--ICTs are a major factor in determining type of information available and how the information is used and consequently how the organization operates. E.g. email and videoconferencing now facilitate communication

--Organizations are becoming flatter and leaner because top management are able to monitor operations more directly, and computers are helping to make decisions previously taken by middle management, e.g. loan authorization in banks, etc.

--Centralization versus decentralization. ICTs systems with distributed data processing enable more centralization of standards and policies and more decentralization of operations e.g. speedy service to customers.

 

For organizational risk

--security of information systems, especially use of intranets and extranets. (covered more fully in lecture on systems security)

Ethical Implications of Information Systems

--Ethics refer to the principles of right and wrong that individuals, acting as free moral agents, use to make choices to guide their behaviours. These are embodied in a system of principles, rules or standards that define certain values - good and bad, right and wrong – and which govern the conduct of a person or group in a general or specific situation.

--Information systems raise ethical questions and problems because they create opportunities for intense social change, and thus threaten existing social, political and business fabric.

--IT can be used to achieve social progress, but it can also be used to commit crime

Ethical issues

--Developments in IT have provided benefits for many and costs for others.

--Whether you run your own business or work in a large company, you will be confronted by these issues and you will need to know how to deal with them. For example:

--Ensuring that the finance and accounting IS you work with are protected from fraud

--In manufacturing and production you will need to deal with data quality and software problems that could interrupt the smooth and accurate flow of information among disparate manufacturing and production systems and among supply chain partners.

 --Ethical issues have been given new urgency by the rise of the Internet and electronic commerce. These technologies make it easier to assemble, integrate, and distribute information, unleashing new concerns about moral dimensions in the information age, e.g.::

 

– Information rights and obligations, including the appropriate usage of customer information such as credit and financial information.

 

--The protection of intellectual property (IP), especially in the age of automation

– Establishing responsibility, accountability, and liability for the consequences of ISs. Question is: if a person is injured by a machine controlled in part by software, who should be held accountable, and therefore liable?

– Setting standards to safeguard system quality that protect the safety of people and society.

– The protection of personal privacy – including tax and education data.

– Health issues – exposure to elements that impact negatively on health, such as repetitive stress syndrome and techno stress.

– Working conditions – e.g. electronic monitoring systems on employees to guard against sending and receiving frivolous mail, etc.

– Computer crime

 

Ethics is a concern of humans who have freedom of choice. Ethics is about individual choice: When faced with alternative courses of action, what is the correct moral choice? What are the main features of ethical choice?

 

Ethical Principles

1.Do unto others as you would have them do unto you (the Golden Rule). Putting yourself into the place of others, and thinking of yourself as the object of the decision, can help you think about fairness in decision making.

2.If an action is not right for everyone to take, it is not right for anyone (Immanuel Kant’s Categorical Imperative). Ask yourself, ―If everyone did this, could the organization, or society, survive?‖

3.If an action cannot be taken repeatedly, it is not right to take at all (Descartes’ rule of change). This is the slippery-slope rule: An action may bring about a small change now that is acceptable, but if it is repeated, it would bring unacceptable changes in the long run.

4.Take the action that achieves the higher or greater value (the Utilitarian Principle). This rule assumes you can prioritize values in a rank order and understand the consequences of various courses of action.

5.Take the action that produces the least harm or the least potential cost (Risk Aversion Principle). Some actions have extremely high failure costs of very low probability (e.g., building a nuclear generating facility in an urban area

6.Assume that virtually all tangible and intangible objects are owned by someone else unless there is a specific declaration otherwise. (This is the ethical ―no free lunch rule.) If something someone else has created is useful to you, it has value, and you should assume the creator wants compensation for this work

 Managing Change

--Management of change is a critical element in organizational development and success

In today's rapidly changing, highly competitive environment, the ability to change rapidly, efficiently, and almost continually will distinguish the winners from the losers

It is not the strongest of the species that survives, nor the most intelligent, but the one most responsive to change. —Charles Darwin

Organizational Change

--Change occurs in many ways: legislative, competitive pressures, operating environment, changing client/consumer preferences, introduction of new technology, etc.

--Organizational change normally involves some threat, real or perceived, of personal loss for those involved.

--Managing change involves leading people on a different path than what they are used to.

--It involves a structured approach to change in individuals, teams, organizations and societies that enables the transition from a current state to a desired or anticipated future state

--Apart from dealing with change itself, managers must also cope with resistance to change encountered from staff, clients, customers, etc

 

Types of Change

--Operational changes affect the way the ongoing operations of the business are conducted.

--Strategic changes occur in the strategic business direction.

--Cultural changes affect the basic organizational philosophies by which the business is conducted, e.g., implementing performance contracts.

--Political changes occur mostly in staffing usually for political reasons

 

Technological Change

--Technological change cuts across all other types of changes discussed above.

--As information systems are designed, developed, and implemented, they affect people and organizations.

--In turn, the major challenges to system success are often more behavioral than technical. Thus, people who have low psychological ownership in a system and who vigorously resist its implementation can bring a “technically best” system to its knees.--Managers need to know how to manage the organizational impact of information systems so as to reduce the resistance to technological change and achieve a more rapid and productive introduction of ISs.

Managers should not make decisions to change technologies without due consultation and putting in place methods to manage the impending change

 Resistance to change

--Resistance to change is an ongoing problem because it impairs efforts to improve performance.

--An organization is a complex system of relationships between people, technologies, and work processes. From this interaction emerge organizational behavior, culture, and performance.

--Understanding these organizational dynamics is a prerequisite for leading effective change processes

The Role of Management in Managing

Resistance to Change

--Management's first responsibility is assess the need for change

--Also estimate what impact a change will likely have on employee behaviour patterns, work processes, technological requirements, and motivation.

--Assess what employee reactions will be and craft a change program that will provide support as workers go through the process of accepting change.

--The program must then be implemented, disseminated throughout the organization, monitored for effectiveness, and adjusted where necessary.

--Also, an important role for management is dealing with resistance to change

Other ways of managing change

 Overcoming Resistance to Change

--Education and Communication-communicating the logic of a change can reduce employee resistance

--Participation-It’s difficult to resist a change decision in which staff participated. Their involvement can reduce resistance, obtain commitment, and increase the quality of the change decision.

--Building Support and Commitment-Employees area also more accepting of changes when they are committed to the organizationas a whole. So, firing up employees and emphasizing their commitment to the organization overall can also help them emotionally commit to the change rather than embrace the status quo.

--Develop Positive Relationships-People are more willing to accept changes if they trust the managers implementing them

--Implementing Changes Fairly-One way organizations can minimize negative impact is to make sure change is implemented fairly. it’s crucial that employees see the reason for the change and perceive its implementation as consistent and fair

--Manipulation and Cooptation-Manipulation refers to covert influence attempts. Twisting facts to make them more attractive, withholding information,and creating false rumors to get employees to accept change are all examples of manipulation.

--Selecting People Who Accept Change-organizations can facilitate change by selecting people who easily to accept change

--Coercion-the application of direct threats or force on the resisters. Other examples are threats of transfer, loss of promotions, negative performance evaluations, and a poor letter of recommendation
