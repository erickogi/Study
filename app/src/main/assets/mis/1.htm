INTRODUCTION TO INFORMATION SYSTEMS

Every day of our lives, we are part of a system, either directly or indirectly. Moreover, modern management philosophies have become irreversibly intertwined with the computer. Organizational concepts, strategy and decision making increasingly depend on flow of information which is increasingly generated by the modern digital computer. Computers in organizations are no longer a prestige symbol, but essential tools.

 

The study of MIS arose in the 1970s to focus on the computer-based information systems aimed at managers.

MIS combines the work of computer science, management science, operations research, as well as sociology, economics and psychology with practical orientation toward building systems. i.e. a social-technical systems perspective which helps to avoid a purely technological approach to information systems

It is therefore imperative that we clearly understand our roles within systems, be more effective as targets of a system, and know when and what kind of systems to use.

Definition of terms

Management. The process of getting activities completed efficiently with and through other people, through the execution of basic management functions which include:

Planning, Organizing, Directing, Controlling, and Coordinating.

In addition, the function of decision-making takes place within each of the above categories.

Management in organizations mainly has 3 levels: top, middle and operational.

Different types of decisions are made at each level and each level therefore has different information requirements.

Information. Term with many meanings depending on context. It is data that has been interpreted and understood and given meaning within a given context.

 

Information performs several functions in organizations, including:

-Reduction of uncertainty especially in planning and decision-making

-Helps in monitoring and control

-As a means of communication.

-Aids in simplification so that problems and situations are better understood and become more manageable.

-Managers often accept computer generated information as gospel truth. Sometimes this can be disastrous; it is important to evaluate the quality of information generated by information systems.

-Characteristics/qualities of (good/useful) information include: Accuracy and verifiability; Completeness; Timeliness; Relevance; Understandability and appropriateness of format; Economical; Accessibility, etc.

System. Has many definitions, but we can define it as:

a group of independent but interrelated elements/components/parts comprising a unified whole and working together toward a common goal by accepting inputs and producing outputs in an organized transformation process

-This definition emphasizes the fact that a system is an assembly of parts/components where:

-The parts are connected together in an organized way

-The parts are affected by being in the system (and are changed by leaving it)

-The assembly does something for a purpose

Systems may be open or closed.

Open system -one which interacts with its external environment.

Closed system -does not interact with surrounding environment. This can only apply to mechanical and physical systems, e.g. the production sub-system where arrangements are made to limit exchanges with external environments so that operations can flow smoothly

. Information system. A general term for a system comprising people, machines, hardware, software, procedures communications, policies, and/or methods organized to collect, process, store, and disseminate information in order to support decision-making.

Contemporary approaches to information systems

-As noted earlier systems, especially contemporary information systems are social-technical systems, viewed from a multidisciplinary perspective

In general the fild of information systems can be divided into technical and behavioral approaches, as illustrated below:

-Technical Approaches

-Computer Science

-Operations Research

-MIS

-Management Science

-Sociology

-Psychology

-Economics

-Behavioural Approaches

Technical approaches

-It emphasizes mathematically based models to study information systems as well as physical technology and formal capabilities of the systems. The disciplines that contribute to this approach are:

:Computer science -concerned with establishing computation theories and methods of efficient data storage and access.

:Management science -concerned with development of models of decision-making and management practices

: Operations research -concerned with mathematical technologies for optimizing selected parameters of organizations such as transportation, inventory control and transaction costs

Behavioral approach

It is concerned with issues that arise in the development and long-term maintenance of information systems, e.g. design, implementation, and utilization. It concentrates on change in attitude, management and behavior. Contributing disciplines are:

Sociology -study information systems with a view on how people and organizations shape the development of systems and how such systems affect them

Psychology - study information systems with an interest in how information is perceived and used by human decision-makers

Economists - study information systems with an interest in what impact the systems have on control and cost structures within and outside the organizations

Information system therefore refers to technology-based system that does two things:

-Provides information processing capabilities for an individual or entire company. The processing capabilities refers to the system’s ability to handle and process information

-Provides information that people need to make better, more informed decisions. Information systems provide decision-makers with on-demand reports and inquiry capabilities as well as routine periodic reports and feedback.

-The main objective of an information system is to monitor/document the operations of some other system, called a target system, e.g. human resources in a firm would be the target system of a human resource information system.

-An information system has the same four capabilities as any other computer system, i.e.: Input in various forms; Processing; and Output in a variety of formats. These constitute the three most important activities that an information system carry out which produce the information that organizations need for making decisions.

-Input : captures or collects raw data from within or without the organization

-Processing : converts raw data input into a more meaningful form

-Output :transfers the processed information to the people who will use it or the activities for which it will be used.

-In addition there is feedback which is the output that is returned to the appropriate members of the organization to help them evaluate or correct the input stage

Rationale/drivers of IS

Some of the main drivers of IS in organizations today include:

a)Sustaining business competitiveness. Every employee must know how to take advantage of ISs to improve their organizations and to leverage the available information into a competitive advantage for the organizations.

b)globalization of economies. Information systems allow firms to have a global reach. Firms must understand capabilities of ISs and how to use them in the global economy.

C)Transformation of industrial economies. Today, knowledge and information are key ingredients in creating wealth. From the agricultural to the industrial economy to the information economy. Different industries are taking advantage of the information systems/technological explosion by using computers to increase efficiency, effectiveness, productivity, and to reduce workloads, etc

d)Transformation of business enterprise. Technology, to a large extent, has driven organizations to change the way they operate and manage. They are leaner, more efficient; more flatter structures. These changes may lead to job cuts, etc. but by learning to use IS employees still remain relevant in their employment.

e) Developments in digitization. Transformation of analog data into digital data for computer storage and processing by a variety of means has led to more effective and efficient input, output, processing and storage capabilities. This in turn has lead to increase in productivity and reduction in costs.

f) Separating work from location, i.e. teleworking

g)Reorganizing workflows, i.e. progressively replacing manual work procedures with automated procedures.

h)Increasing flexibility of organizations, e.g. ability to respond to changes in the marketplace

 

Management Information Requirements

at Different Levels

Operational managers

-Deals intensively with internal information;

-Effective and efficient information processing is important since controls are numerous, monitoring is constant, data volumes are high and a rapid response is needed;

-Information for structured decision-making, i.e. easily defined information for example stock taking summary statistics, reports etc. they therefore require structured information

 

Tactical managers

The span of management control is wider than at operational level, e.g. periodic and what-if scenarios, operational and budgeting, quality control issues, resources allocation, etc, and therefore, information must be drawn from a larger number of different sources;

Managers face complexity and uncertainty and require judgment, insight and good interpersonal skills;

The environment is diverse and numerous variables must be taken into account in the decision making process and data may be required on all of them;

Key information is concerned with the implementation of strategic objectives and monitoring of operations;

Strategic managers

Concerned with new and unstructured decision-making situations, non-repetitive, and filled with uncertainty, e.g. external factors such as markets

Information for strategic decision-making and dealing with policy issues

Strategic information is largely external to the organization and key issues include competitor performance and actions, economic tends, technology changes and market changes;

Largely used to predict the future, so therefore, trends, forecasts and assessments are vital;

Qualitative as well as quantitative information is important;

Need summary level information such as total company salaries and sales.

Information required is boundary free, i.e. it is not confirmed to a functional area or activity. It must reflect a holistic view.

So what are Management Information System

There are many dimensions of MIS and no simple definition is yet adequate. Obviously, a background in systems studies is necessary to fully appreciate the meaning of MIS. A formal definition of MIS is:

A comprehensive and coordinated set of information sub-systems which are rationally integrated and which transform data into information in a variety of ways to enhance productivity in conformance with manager’s styles and characteristics.

This definition can be expanded as follows:

a) MIS is Comprehensive. It includes information systems primarily designed for managers at all levels

b) MIS is Coordinated. This ensures that all parts – data processing, office automation, etc are developed and operated in planned and coordinated way for efficient operation

c) MIS has Information Sub-systems. Some of the sub-systems serve just one activity or level in the firm, while others may serve more levels and activities. These sub-systems are rationally integrated so that their activities are interrelated; they must all share the goals of the MIS and the organization.

d) MIS Transform Data into Information in a Variety of Ways. The transformation is determined by the characteristics of the organization’s personnel, the task for which the information is intended, etc.

e)MIS Enhances Productivity, e.g. it enables routine tasks such as document preparation to be carried out more efficiently, enhances the manager’s ability to deal with anticipated problems, etc.

f) MIS Conforms with Manager’s Styles and Characteristics, e.g. at strategic level, the IS is likely to be tailored to an individual manager’s tastes; for professional personnel, IS is tailored to the nature of specialized tasks and how they process information; at the lower levels IS is likely to be tailored to the usual way in which operations personnel use information and interact with the information system.

Influence on MIS Design

Major factors which influence the structure and scope of an organization’s MIS are outlined below:

a) Primary function of the organization. Is it a manufacturing or service organization, a Describe the hospital, etc? Are there numerous functions? What linkages exist between them?

b) Structure and levels of the organization. Does organization have many departments, branches? What degree of autonomy do the sections, departments have?

c) Degree of centralization/decentralization. Is organization tightly controlled at the centre? What decisions/actions are the sections allowed to take?

d) Interaction with environment. To what extent is external environment important to the organization, in terms of customers, suppliers, government departments? What is the legislative framework?

e) Decision-making. What sort of decisions need to be taken? Who makes the decisions?

f) Scale of operations. What is the volume of trade or business transactions?

g) Use of IT. What is the extent and type of IT infrastructure used or available? What degree of expertise and skills exists?

h) Management style. Is it an authoritarian or participative style? Are procedures and operations routine closely prescribed? What is the control structure?

Problems with MIS

Failure of MISs in providing management with information they need maybe as a result of:

-Lack of management involvement with design of the MIS; lack of top management support

-Narrow and/or inappropriate emphasis of the computer system

-Concentration on low-level data processing applications particularly in the accounting area

-Lack of management knowledge of computers

-Poor appreciation by information specialists of manager’s true information requirements and of organizational problems
